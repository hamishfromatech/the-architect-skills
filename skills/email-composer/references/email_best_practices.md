# Email Best Practices Reference

## Subject Line Guidelines

### Characteristics of Good Subject Lines
- **Clear**: Immediately conveys the purpose
- **Specific**: Includes relevant details (project name, date, urgency)
- **Actionable**: Indicates if action is required
- **Concise**: Under 50 characters when possible

### Subject Line Patterns

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Request | [Action Required] Topic | [Action Required] Q4 Budget Approval |
| Update | Status: Topic - Date | Status: Website Redesign - Nov 15 |
| Meeting | Meeting: Topic - Date | Meeting: Q4 Planning - Nov 20 |
| Urgent | URGENT: Topic | URGENT: Production Server Down |
| FYI | FYI: Topic | FYI: New HR Policy Update |

### What to Avoid
- Vague subjects: "Hi", "Question", "Update"
- ALL CAPS (except for urgency)
- Excessive punctuation!!!
- Misleading subject lines

## Email Body Structure

### Opening Paragraph
- State purpose clearly in first 1-2 sentences
- Include relevant context if needed
- Keep it concise

### Body Paragraphs
- One main idea per paragraph
- Use bullet points for lists
- Bold key information
- Keep paragraphs short (3-5 sentences)

### Closing Paragraph
- State next steps clearly
- Include call to action
- Set expectations for response time
- Offer additional help

## Tone Guidelines

### Formal Tone
**When to use:**
- First-time contacts
- Senior executives
- External clients
- Sensitive topics
- Legal/official matters

**Characteristics:**
- Complete sentences
- No contractions
- Professional sign-offs
- Respectful language

**Example:**
> Dear Dr. Thompson,
>
> I am writing to formally request your review of the attached proposal.

### Semi-Formal Tone
**When to use:**
- Regular clients
- Established colleagues
- Day-to-day business

**Characteristics:**
- Some contractions acceptable
- Professional but approachable
- Standard sign-offs

**Example:**
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I wanted to follow up on our discussion from yesterday's meeting.

### Casual Tone
**When to use:**
- Close team members
- Quick internal updates
- Established relationships

**Characteristics:**
- Informal greetings
- Contractions common
- Brief sign-offs
- Can use emojis sparingly

**Example:**
> Hey team,
>
> Quick update on the project status - we're on track!

## Common Email Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Unclear Purpose
❌ "I wanted to touch base about some things."
✅ "I'm writing to discuss the Q4 budget allocation for the marketing team."

### 2. Buried Call to Action
❌ "Let me know what you think when you get a chance, no rush."
✅ "Please respond by Friday with your approval or questions."

### 3. Information Overload
❌ Long paragraphs with multiple topics
✅ Clear sections with headers and bullet points

### 4. Passive Voice Overuse
❌ "It was decided that the report should be submitted."
✅ "The team decided to submit the report."

### 5. Missing Context
❌ "Can you review this?"
✅ "Can you review the attached Q4 financial projections by Wednesday? We need your input for the board presentation."

## Response Time Guidelines

| Priority | Expected Response |
|----------|------------------|
| Urgent | Within 1 hour |
| High | Same business day |
| Medium | Within 24-48 hours |
| Low | Within 1 week |

## Email Etiquette Quick Reference

### TO Field
- Primary recipients who need to take action
- People directly involved in the topic

### CC Field
- People who should be informed but don't need to act
- Stakeholders who need visibility

### BCC Field
- Use for mass emails to protect privacy
- Never use for hidden communication

### Reply vs Reply All
- Reply: Only the sender needs your response
- Reply All: Everyone on the thread needs the information

## Attachment Best Practices

1. **Name files clearly**: `Q4_Report_Draft_Nov15.pdf`
2. **Mention attachments in body**: "Please find attached..."
3. **Check file size**: Keep under 10MB when possible
4. **Use appropriate formats**: PDF for final documents
5. **Compress multiple files**: Use ZIP for multiple attachments
